Organic Compounds

Organic Compounds that are nonpolar and exhibit weak intermolecular forces have low melting points and are nonelectrolytes.

As the number of carbon atoms in a hydrocarbon series decreases the normal boiling point decreases.

The lower the amount of carbons, the lower the normal boiling point

The bonds between atoms in an organic molecule are normally covalent.

Covalent bonds are the sharing of electrons between two or more atoms.

Characteristics of Organic Compounds

- Low Melting Point

- Insoluable in Water

- Decomposed Under High Heat

- Nonelectrolyte

Inorganic and Organic Reactions

Organic reactions are usually slower because the particles are molecules.

Inorganic reactions are usually faster because the particles are ionic.

Isomers

Isomers are each of two or more compounds with the same formula but a different arrangement of atoms in the molecule and different properties.

Isomers differ in molecular structure

Saturated and Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Saturated hydrocarbons only have single bond.

Saturated hydrocarbons are known as alkanes.

In the alkane family each member differs by one carbon atom and two hydrogen atoms (CH2) this continuous pattern or series of hydrocarbons is known as a homologous series.

Unsaturated hydrocarbons have more than a single bond.

Unsaturated hydrocarbons are known as alkenes or alkynes.

Substitution, Fermentation, Esterfication, Polymerization, and Saponification

Substitution

A chemical reaction during which one functional group in a chemical compound is replaced by another functional group.

Fermentation

The chemical breakdown of a substance by bacteria, yeasts, or other microorganisms, typically involving effervescence and the giving off of heat.

Esterfication

A reaction of an alcohol with an acid to produce an ester and water.

Polymerization

The joining of multiple monomers, or the chaining together of small molecules to produce a large molecule.

Saponification

A chemical reaction between an acid and a base to form a salt.
